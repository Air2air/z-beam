DESCRIPTION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Ceramic-glaze contamination consists of vitrified silicate
residues that form during high-temperature firing in ceramic
manufacturing, so layer adheres tightly to surfaces after
cooling. This contamination shows unique glassy hardness and
chemical inertness, which distinguish it from softer organic
residues or metallic scales, and on materials like porcelain or
alumina, it behaves as a uniform coating that resists cracking
under stress. Removal faces challenges from its durable bond,
but laser cleaning proves effective because precise energy
pulses ablate the glaze selectively, so substrate remains
undamaged during treatment.
