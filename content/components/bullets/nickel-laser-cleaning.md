- **[APPLICATIONS]** Laser cleaning of nickel components is extensively employed within the European precision engineering sector for the restoration of turbine blades and the selective removal of oxides from sensitive electroplating fixtures prior to recoating processes. This non-abrasive technique is particularly valued for maintaining the substrate's dimensional integrity, a critical requirement in high-value manufacturing applications across the region.

- **[PARAMETERS]** Optimal cleaning of nickel typically employs a fiber laser with a 1,070 nm wavelength, utilizing fluences carefully controlled between 1.5 and 3.5 J/cm² to effectively ablate contaminants without inducing microstructural changes to the underlying material. Shorter pulse durations in the nanosecond range are generally preferred to minimize the total heat input and ensure a precise, cold ablation process that complies with stringent quality protocols.

- **[ENVIRONMENTAL]** The process eliminates the need for chemical solvents and abrasive media, significantly reducing hazardous waste generation and aligning with the waste minimization objectives of the European Green Deal for sustainable industrial practices. This dry technique also allows for the easy containment and filtration of removed particulates, contributing to a cleaner workshop environment and simplifying compliance with local environmental regulations.

- **[SAFETY]** Strict adherence to EN 60825-1 for laser safety is mandatory, requiring the use of appropriate wavelength-specific interlocks and personal protective equipment for all operators to prevent ocular and dermal exposure to the reflected beam. Furthermore, the process necessitates the implementation of robust fume extraction systems to capture and filter potentially harmful nickel oxide nanoparticles that are aerosolized during the ablation process.

- **[CHALLENGES]** A primary difficulty involves the precise calibration of laser parameters to remove tenacious oxides without affecting the nickel substrate's inherent surface finish or inducing any localized melting that could compromise fatigue life. This is often mitigated through the use of sophisticated scanning systems and real-time process monitoring to guarantee consistent results and uphold the material's properties.
