- **[APPLICATIONS]** Laser cleaning excels in restoring historical granite monuments and preparing architectural surfaces within the European stone industry, where precision and non-abrasive material preservation are paramount. This method is also extensively applied for pre-treatment surface preparation in high-value manufacturing sectors, ensuring optimal adhesion for subsequent processes.

- **[PARAMETERS]** Optimal cleaning of granite typically employs a near-infrared wavelength, such as 1064 nm, with a fluence carefully maintained between 0.5 and 1.5 J/cm² to effectively remove contaminants without altering the stone's mineralogical composition. A short pulse duration in the nanosecond range is generally favored to efficiently ablate surface deposits through a thermo-mechanical process while minimizing any thermal load on the substrate.

- **[ENVIRONMENTAL]** This process eliminates the need for chemical solvents and generates minimal waste, predominantly comprising the ablated particulate matter which can be effectively captured by integrated filtration systems. This aligns with stringent European environmental directives, significantly reducing the ecological footprint compared to traditional abrasive or chemical cleaning methodologies.

- **[SAFETY]** Strict adherence to safety protocols, including the use of certified laser safety eyewear specific to the operating wavelength and ensuring proper workstation enclosure, is mandatory to protect operators from scattered radiation. Comprehensive extraction and ventilation systems are also critical to mitigate inhalation risks from the generated aerosolized particles, in compliance with regional occupational health standards.

- **[CHALLENGES]** A primary difficulty involves the heterogeneous composition of granite, where different minerals like quartz and feldspar exhibit varying absorption coefficients, potentially leading to uneven cleaning results if parameters are not meticulously optimized. This is mitigated through systematic parameter testing on inconspicuous areas and the use of advanced scanning systems to ensure uniform energy distribution across the complex surface.
