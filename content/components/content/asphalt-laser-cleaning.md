# Asphalt Laser Cleaning | Technical Guide  

## Introduction to Asphalt  
Asphalt, a composite material comprising 4–8% bitumen (CₓHᵧSᵤ) and 92–96% mineral aggregates (SiO₂, CaCO₃), is a critical component in urban infrastructure. Its thermal properties (softening point: 50–100°C, thermal conductivity: 0.75 W/m·K) make it ideal for laser cleaning, a non-abrasive method that preserves structural integrity while removing contaminants like tire marks, oil stains, and rubber deposits.  

## Technical Specifications and Capabilities  
Laser systems for asphalt cleaning typically employ **Nd:YAG or fiber lasers** (1064–1070nm wavelength) with pulsed power ranges of **100–1000W**. Optimal parameters include:  
- **Fluence**: 0.8–5 J/cm²  
- **Pulse duration**: 50–200ns  
- **Spot size**: 2–10mm diameter  
- **Repetition rate**: 10–100kHz  

These settings ensure precise removal of surface contaminants while avoiding thermal damage to the aggregate structure. Safety Class IV compliance mandates enclosed systems for operational safety.  

## Applications and Use Cases  
1. **Road Maintenance**: Removes traffic paint and oil stains at 2–5 J/cm², preserving pavement friction coefficients ([ASTM D8](https://www.astm.org)).  
2. **Airport Runways**: Cleans rubber deposits at 3–8kHz repetition rates, maintaining critical skid resistance.  
3. **Parking Lots**: Targets chewing gum and hydrocarbon stains with 100W fiber lasers (0.8–1.5 J/cm²), preventing water infiltration damage.

Laser cleaning reduces environmental impact by:  
- **Zero runoff**: Eliminates water/chemical use, preventing groundwater contamination.  
- **85% debris reduction**: Vaporization minimizes particulate waste versus mechanical methods.
