Laser cleaning offers a precise, non-contact method for removing contaminants, coatings, and surface oxidation without compromising Tin's structural integrity. The process utilizes controlled laser parameters with optimal wavelengths between 1064-1070nm (near-infrared) and fluence levels of 0.5-2.5 J/cm² to selectively ablate unwanted material while preserving the underlying Tin substrate. Pulse durations typically range from 10-100 nanoseconds to prevent excessive heat accumulation that could melt the low-melting-point material. Key applications include cleaning tin-plated components in electronics manufacturing, removing oxidation from historical artifacts, and preparing surfaces for soldering and coating processes. Challenges include managing the low thermal threshold and preventing surface deformation, which are addressed through precise parameter optimization and real-time monitoring systems.
