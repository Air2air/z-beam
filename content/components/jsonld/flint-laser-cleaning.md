Its microcrystalline structure with Mohs hardness of 7 makes it resistant to mechanical abrasion but susceptible to thermal shock if improper laser parameters are applied. Optimal laser cleaning parameters for Flint typically utilize near-infrared wavelengths (1064 nm) with moderate fluence levels (2-8 J/cm²) and short pulse durations (10-100 ns) to selectively remove contaminants, biological growth, and surface deposits without causing thermal damage to the underlying material. Key applications include archaeological artifact restoration, architectural conservation of historic Flint buildings, industrial tool maintenance, and preparation of Flint surfaces for analytical testing. The primary challenge involves Flint's variable composition and potential for thermal fracturing, which requires precise control of laser parameters and scanning patterns. Safety considerations include proper fume extraction due to potential silica particulate release, comprehensive eye protection against reflected laser radiation, and controlled environment maintenance to prevent accidental exposure.
