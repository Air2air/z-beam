Its cellular structure consists of approximately 45% cellulose, 28% lignin, and 20% hemicellulose, with natural tannins and extractives providing inherent weather resistance. Laser cleaning offers a revolutionary approach for surface treatment, utilizing precisely controlled near-infrared wavelengths (1064 nm) with fluence levels between 0.8-1.5 J/cm² and pulse durations of 100-150 ns. This parameter range effectively removes surface contaminants, biological growth, and previous coatings while minimizing thermal penetration to preserve the wood's structural integrity. Key applications include restoration of historical structures, preparation of architectural components, and conservation of cultural artifacts where traditional abrasive methods would cause damage. The primary challenge involves the material's variable density and natural resin content, which requires dynamic parameter adjustment to prevent charring or uneven cleaning. Advanced scanning systems with real-time monitoring ensure consistent results across the entire surface. Safety protocols mandate Class 1 laser enclosure systems, proper ventilation for particulate management, and operator training for hazardous material handling.
