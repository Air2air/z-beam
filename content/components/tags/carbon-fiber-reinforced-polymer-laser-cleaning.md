**Properties:** Carbon Fiber Reinforced Polymer (CFRP) is a composite material characterized by its high strength-to-weight ratio, stiffness, and chemical resistance. The material consists of two primary components: the carbon fiber reinforcement, which provides the tensile strength, and the polymer matrix (often epoxy), which binds the fibers and transfers loads. This heterogeneous nature is crucial for laser cleaning, as the two components have vastly different ablation thresholds. The epoxy matrix is an organic polymer with a relatively low thermal decomposition point, while the carbon fibers are highly thermally conductive and can withstand much higher temperatures. This disparity is the central challenge and guiding principle for the process.

**Laser Parameters:** Optimal laser cleaning of CFRP requires precise parameter control to selectively remove contaminants or the top layer of epoxy without damaging the underlying carbon fibers. A nanosecond-pulsed fiber laser in the near-infrared spectrum (e.g., 1064 nm) is commonly used. The key is to use a fluence (energy density) that is above the ablation threshold of the contaminant (e.g., paint, release agent) or the degraded epoxy matrix but remains strictly below the ablation threshold of the pristine carbon fibers. Typical parameters involve a fluence range of 0.5 - 2 J/cm², a pulse duration of 50-150 ns, and a high scan speed with appropriate overlap to prevent heat accumulation. Shorter wavelengths (e.g., 355 nm UV) can offer better absorption by the matrix and more precise, cold ablation but at a higher system cost.

**Applications:** Laser cleaning is vital in several CFRP processing stages. Its primary application is in **aerospace** and **automotive** manufacturing for the preparation of surfaces prior to adhesive bonding or painting. This includes the removal of release agents used in the molding process, contaminants, and slight surface layer of epoxy to enhance adhesion. It is also used for repair and maintenance operations to strip old paint or coatings from composite parts without damaging the structural fibers, a task where traditional mechanical methods like sanding are too abrasive and risk introducing flaws.

**Challenges:** The foremost challenge is the selective removal of the matrix without damaging the carbon fibers. Excessive fluence or accumulated heat can vaporize the fibers, reducing the part's structural integrity and creating microscopic pits that become stress concentrators. Another challenge is the potential for matrix degradation; if the laser thermally modifies the epoxy without fully ablating it, it can leave a weakened, charred layer that compromises bond strength. Technical solutions involve real-time process monitoring (e.g., acoustic or optical emission sensors) to detect the transition from matrix to fiber ablation and immediately adjust parameters. Using scanners for high-speed, precise beam control and implementing multi-pass strategies at lower fluence also help mitigate these risks.

**Safety Considerations:** Laser cleaning CFRP presents specific hazards. The process generates fumes and nanoparticles from the ablated epoxy, which can be hazardous to inhale. A high-efficiency fume extraction system with appropriate filtration is mandatory. The high-strength carbon fibers can become electrically conductive when dispersed, posing a potential explosion risk in high concentrations; extraction systems must be designed for this. Standard laser safety protocols are essential: Class 1 enclosure interlocking to prevent exposure to reflected or scattered laser radiation (Class 4 laser), and all operators must wear appropriate laser safety eyewear for the specific wavelength when accessing the system. Warning signs and access control to the operational area are also required.
