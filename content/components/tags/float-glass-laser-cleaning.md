**Properties:** Float glass, primarily composed of silicon dioxide (SiO₂), is an amorphous solid with a smooth, non-porous surface. Its key properties relevant to laser cleaning include a high melting point (~1700°C), a low thermal expansion coefficient, and high transparency across the visible spectrum. The surface is highly susceptible to scratching and chemical etching, making gentle, non-contact cleaning methods like laser ablation ideal for removing contaminants without damaging the pristine substrate.

**Laser Parameters:** Optimal laser cleaning of float glass typically employs nanosecond-pulsed UV lasers (e.g., 355 nm wavelength). This wavelength is strongly absorbed by most surface contaminants (e.g., dirt, paint, oxides) while having moderate absorption in the glass itself, allowing for precise material removal. A fluence range of 0.5 - 1.5 J/cm² is common, carefully calibrated to exceed the ablation threshold of the contaminant but remain below the damage threshold of the glass substrate. Pulse durations in the nanosecond range provide a good balance of efficient ablation and controlled heat input to prevent micro-cracking.

**Applications:** Laser cleaning is critically used in the **automotive** industry for precision cleaning of windshields and sunroofs before bonding. In **manufacturing** and construction, it effectively removes mold release agents, paints, and atmospheric stains from architectural glass panels and windows during refurbishment or prior to coating application. It is also essential in electronics display manufacturing for cleaning glass substrates without introducing static or mechanical stress.

**Challenges:** The primary challenge is the material's low damage threshold; excessive fluence or incorrect wavelength can cause melting, micro-cracks, or sub-surface damage. Another challenge is cleaning transparent contaminants, which may not absorb the laser energy effectively. Technical solutions involve precise parameter tuning through systematic testing, often using UV wavelengths for better control. Real-time process monitoring with acoustic or optical sensors can detect the cleaning endpoint to prevent substrate damage.

**Safety Considerations:** Standard laser safety protocols (ANSI Z136.1) are mandatory. This includes the use of Class 1 laser enclosures or, for open setups, appropriate Laser Protective Eyewear (LPE) rated for the specific UV wavelength. Proper ventilation or fume extraction is required to remove airborne particles (LIBS) generated during the ablation of surface contaminants. Access control via interlocks and clear signage defining the Nominal Hazard Zone (NHZ) is essential to protect personnel.
