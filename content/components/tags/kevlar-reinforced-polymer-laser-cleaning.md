**Properties:** Kevlar-Reinforced Polymer (KRP) is a composite material characterized by its exceptional strength-to-weight ratio, high tensile strength, and thermal stability. The key component, aramid fibers (Kevlar®), is highly resistant to impact and abrasion but is also sensitive to UV radiation and excessive heat. The polymer matrix, often epoxy or phenolic, can degrade, discolor, or char if subjected to thermal overload. These properties necessitate a highly controlled cleaning process to remove contaminants (e.g., mold release agents, paints, oxides) without damaging the underlying reinforcing fibers or the matrix integrity.

**LaserParameters:** Optimal laser cleaning of KRP requires precise parameter control to ablate surface contaminants while preserving the substrate. A nanosecond-pulsed fiber laser in the near-infrared (NIR) spectrum (e.g., 1064 nm) is commonly used, as its parameters are easily tuned. The critical parameter is fluence (energy density), which must be carefully set above the ablation threshold of the contaminant but below the damage threshold of the Kevlar fibers and polymer matrix. Typical fluence ranges from 0.5 to 2.5 J/cm². A shorter pulse duration (e.g., 100-150 ns) helps minimize heat conduction into the substrate, reducing the risk of thermal degradation. High repetition rates (kHz range) and optimized scan speeds ensure efficient cleaning without cumulative heat buildup.

**Applications:** Laser cleaning is vital for preparing and maintaining KRP components in demanding industries. In **aerospace**, it is used to remove release agents from newly molded composite parts, strip paint from fuselage and wing components for inspection and repainting, and clean joints for adhesive bonding. In **automotive** and **marine** applications, it prepares KRP body panels and hull components for repairs and coating applications. The **sporting goods** industry uses it to clean and prepare KRP in protective gear like helmets and body armor before applying new finishes or logos.

**Challenges:** The primary challenge is the disparate ablation thresholds between common contaminants and the KRP substrate. The polymer matrix and especially the aramid fibers are susceptible to thermal damage, including melting, charring, and loss of mechanical strength if overheated. Another challenge is the potential for yellowing or whitening of the Kevlar fibers due to UV exposure from certain laser wavelengths. Technical solutions involve using laser-induced breakdown spectroscopy (LIBS) for real-time monitoring to distinguish between contaminant and substrate, allowing for closed-loop parameter control. Selecting a laser wavelength that the contaminant absorbs more efficiently than the KRP (e.g., 1064 nm or 10.6 µm) is also crucial.

**SafetyConsiderations:** Laser cleaning of KRP generates particulates and gases that can be hazardous. The ablation plume may contain volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from the polymer matrix and fine aramid particles, which are respiratory irritants. Therefore, operation within an enclosed interlocked workstation equipped with high-efficiency particulate air (HEPA) filtration and fume extraction is mandatory. Standard laser safety protocols must be followed: using appropriate laser safety eyewear for the specific wavelength, posting warning signs, and employing protective barriers to prevent exposure to stray reflections. A Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS) for the specific KRP composite should be consulted to identify all potential hazardous byproducts.
