- **[APPLICATIONS]** Laser cleaning of steel components is extensively employed within the European heavy manufacturing sector for the critical pre-treatment of surfaces prior to welding or coating applications, ensuring superior adhesion and joint integrity. This non-abrasive technique is also indispensable for the meticulous restoration of historical steel structures and artworks, preserving their original material properties without inducing substrate damage.

- **[PARAMETERS]** Optimal cleaning of steel surfaces typically employs a fiber laser with a 1,064 nm wavelength, utilizing a fluence range between 1-3 J/cm² and nanosecond pulse durations to effectively ablate contaminants like rust and mill scale while preserving the underlying substrate integrity. These parameters are carefully calibrated to stay well within the material's ablation threshold, a critical consideration for maintaining the precise dimensional tolerances demanded by high-precision Italian automotive and aerospace subcontractors.

- **[ENVIRONMENTAL]** The process eliminates the need for chemical solvents and abrasive media, drastically reducing hazardous waste generation and aligning with the stringent waste management directives of the European Green Deal. This dry process also contributes to a circular economy by allowing for the selective removal of coatings, facilitating the high-quality recycling of steel components within the manufacturing loop.

- **[SAFETY]** Strict adherence to European Standard EN 60825-1 for laser product safety is mandatory, requiring the implementation of Class 1 enclosures or equivalent engineering controls to prevent exposure to hazardous diffuse reflections. Operators must be equipped with appropriate laser safety eyewear with optical density ratings specifically calibrated for the 1064 nm wavelength to mitigate the risk of retinal damage.

- **[CHALLENGES]** A primary technical hurdle involves the complete removal of tenacious, heterogeneous oxide layers without inducing even minor surface etching or micro-melting, which can compromise fatigue performance. This is mitigated through advanced scanning strategies and real-time process monitoring systems that dynamically adjust laser parameters to accommodate variations in surface contamination.
