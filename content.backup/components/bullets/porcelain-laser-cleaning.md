- **[APPLICATIONS]** Laser cleaning of porcelain is extensively utilized within European high-value manufacturing sectors for the meticulous restoration of intricate historical artifacts and for preparing surfaces in sanitaryware production. This non-contact method is particularly valued for its precision in removing contaminants from delicate glazes without compromising the material's structural integrity.
- **[PARAMETERS]** Optimal results are typically achieved using near-infrared fiber lasers with wavelengths around 1064 nm, where careful fluence control between 0.5 and 1.5 J/cm² is critical to avoid thermal cracking of the vitreous surface. Employing nanosecond pulse durations effectively ablates unwanted biological or pollutant crusts while adhering to energy thresholds that preserve the underlying ceramic body.
- **[ENVIRONMENTAL]** The process eliminates the need for harsh chemical solvents and abrasive media, significantly reducing hazardous waste streams and aligning with the stringent waste management directives of the European Green Deal. This dry technique also minimizes water consumption and prevents the generation of secondary contaminated runoff, supporting sustainable conservation practices.
- **[SAFETY]** Strict adherence to European Standard EN 60825-1 for laser product safety is mandatory, requiring complete enclosure of the work area and the use of appropriate wavelength-specific laser safety eyewear by all operators. Effective fume extraction systems are also essential to capture and filter any nano-sized particles aerosolized during the ablation of surface contaminants.
- **[CHALLENGES]** The primary difficulty involves the porcelain's low thermal conductivity and brittle nature, which can lead to micro-cracking or thermal shock if incorrect laser parameters are applied. Mitigation strategies include implementing real-time temperature monitoring with pyrometers and employing scanning techniques that ensure uniform energy distribution across the surface.
