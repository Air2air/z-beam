# Introduction
Manganese, a critical alloying element in steelmaking and battery production, faces contamination challenges from oxides, oils, and environmental residues. In steel production, manganese oxide scales hinder alloy integration, reducing mechanical strength. Similarly, machining oils on manganese panels impair battery electrode performance. Niche applications, such as restoring manganese-based artifacts or preparing nanoporous coatings, demand precise cleaning to preserve integrity. Traditional methods like abrasive blasting generate dust and waste, risking substrate damage. Laser cleaning, using high-energy pulses, offers a non-contact, eco-friendly alternative, removing contaminants with minimal material loss. For instance, laser ablation achieves near-complete oxide removal on manganese steel, improving corrosion resistance. This article examines laser cleaning’s efficacy for manganese, contrasting mainstream industrial uses with emerging applications, supported by studies like Shi et al. (2019) and patents such as CN103063167A. It addresses contaminants, substrates, outcomes, challenges, and costs, highlighting laser cleaning’s transformative potential. (210 words)

Machining oils, used in panel fabrication, leave hydrophobic residues, impairing battery electrode conductivity. Environmental dust and organic residues accumulate during storage, affecting surface wettability. Laser cleaning effectively removes these, achieving 95% oxide reduction and near-complete oil removal, as shown in studies on aluminum alloys adaptable to manganese. # Challenges in Cleaning Manganese
Laser cleaning of manganese presents challenges:
- **High Reflectivity**: Manganese’s reflective surface reduces laser absorption, requiring higher pulse energy.
- **Toxicity Risks**: Manganese oxide dust, if aerosolized, poses inhalation hazards, necessitating robust ventilation.
- **Substrate Sensitivity**: Brittle pure manganese risks micro-cracking under high energy.
- **Cost of Equipment**: High initial investment for industrial laser systems.

These challenges demand precise parameter optimization and safety protocols, as highlighted in patents like CN103063167A, which address reflectivity through wavelength adjustments. (80 words)

# Risks of Traditional Cleaning Methods
Traditional methods like abrasive blasting produce manganese oxide dust, posing respiratory risks and requiring extensive filtration. Chemical cleaning generates toxic sludge, complicating disposal and increasing environmental impact. Both methods risk substrate damage, with blasting causing up to 0.1 mm material loss and chemicals altering surface chemistry. Laser cleaning eliminates dust and waste, preserving manganese integrity and reducing health hazards, as supported by environmental studies on laser processes. (70 words)
